Part 6

a) What is the difference between Phong shading and Phong lighting (the Phong reflection model)?

Phong lighting (reflection model) refers to the mathematical model used to calculate the lighting at a point on a surface. It consists of the ambient, diffuse, and specular components that describe how light interacts with a surface.

Phong shading refers to how the lighting model is applied across a surface. Phong shading involves interpolating normals across a polygon and calculating the Phong lighting model per pixel (in the fragment shader), resulting in smooth shading across the entire surface.

b) What is the difference between flat shading, Gouraud shading, and Phong shading?

Flat Shading:

  • Flat shading computes lighting once per polygon (one normal and one color per face).
  • Pros: Fast, simple, and requires fewer computations.
  • Cons: Looks faceted; transitions between polygons are harsh and unrealistic.

Gouraud Shading:

  • Gouraud shading computes lighting at the vertices and then interpolates colors across the polygon.
  • Pros: Smooth appearance, reduces computation compared to Phong shading.
  • Cons: Highlights may not appear if they don't coincide with a vertex; inaccurate specular highlights.

Phong Shading:

  • Phong shading interpolates the normals across the surface and applies the lighting calculation for each pixel (per-fragment shading).
  • Pros: Produces the most accurate and realistic results; handles highlights very well.
  • Cons: Computationally expensive compared to Gouraud and flat shading.

Conclusion: Phong shading is the best method for simulating highlights because it performs per-pixel lighting calculations, allowing for accurate specular highlights even if they fall between vertices.

c) What is the difference between a directional light and a point light?

  • Directional light: A light source that emits parallel rays of light as if it were infinitely far away (e.g., sunlight). It has a direction but no position.
  • Point light: A light source that emits light in all directions from a specific position in space (e.g., a light bulb). The intensity of light decreases with distance from the source.

d) Does the eye position influence the shading of an object in any way?

Yes, the eye position affects the shading, specifically the specular reflection. The view direction (from the surface point to the camera/eye) is used in calculating the specular term in the Phong reflection model. Specular highlights will shift based on the camera's position relative to the surface and light source.

e) What is the effect of setting the specular term to (0, 0, 0)?

If the specular term is set to (0, 0, 0), there will be no specular reflection, meaning that the surface will appear completely matte (non-shiny). This removes any shiny highlights from the surface.

Increasing the shininess exponent ùõº in the Phong reflection model makes the specular highlight smaller and sharper. A higher shininess value simulates highly reflective and glossy surfaces, such as metal or polished surfaces.

g) In what coordinate space did you compute the lighting?

The lighting in the example code is computed in eye (camera) coordinates. The vertex positions and normals are transformed into camera space before the lighting calculations are applied in the fragment shader.
